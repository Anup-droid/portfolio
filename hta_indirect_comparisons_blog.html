Setting the Scene

The Indirect Comparison Problem in HTA

Imagine two effective oncology drugs — Drug A vs placebo in Trial 1, Drug B vs placebo in Trial 2. The NICE submission requires a head-to-head estimate. But that trial was never run. What do you do?

This is the foundational challenge of indirect treatment comparison (ITC) in Health Technology Assessment. Covariate-adjusted ITCs have become a routine requirement in submissions to NICE, HAS, G-BA and other HTA bodies — but the methodological landscape is contested, evolving, and has real consequences for reimbursement decisions that affect patient access.

The paper by Remiro-Azócar, Heath & Baio (2023) is a scholarly counter-response that clarifies four critical methodological tensions. Rather than declaring a single winner, the authors chart a nuanced map of when each approach performs well, when it fails, and why the debate matters for HTA practice.

Why This Debate Matters

The method you choose in an ITC directly shapes the treatment effect estimate submitted to HTA bodies. A biased estimate — whether from model misspecification or poor extrapolation — can lead to incorrect reimbursement decisions, affecting both patients and healthcare budgets. This is not merely an academic dispute.

The Two Core Paradigms

Before diving into the debate, it helps to understand the two main methodological families:

Weighting

MAIC

Matching-Adjusted Indirect Comparison. Re-weights patients in the trial with individual patient data (IPD) so that their covariate distribution matches the aggregate statistics of the comparator trial. Does not require an outcome model.

✓ Bias-robust · Transparent uncertainty
Outcome Modeling

Parametric G-computation

Fits an outcome model (e.g. linear regression) to IPD, then predicts outcomes under each treatment scenario in the target population. Requires correct specification of the outcome-generating model.

~ Efficient · Extrapolates · Model-dependent
Combined

Doubly-Robust Methods

Augmented weighting estimators that incorporate both a trial assignment model and an outcome model. Unbiased if either model is correctly specified — two bites at the apple.

✓ Most bias-robust · Computationally intensive
Section 01

Bias-Robustness: The Case for Weighting

The most fundamental question any HTA analyst must ask is: if my model is wrong, how wrong will my answer be? This is the question of bias-robustness.

"In parametric G-computation, bias arising from model misspecification cannot be easily verified in the data. An outcome model that seems approximately correct when fitted to the patient-level data would not necessarily fit well in extrapolated regions."

Remiro-Azócar et al., 2023

Why Outcome Model Misspecification is Hard to Detect

The seductive problem with parametric G-computation is that your model can look excellent in-sample while being dangerously wrong where you need it most — in the non-overlapping region of the covariate space. Standard model diagnostics (AIC, BIC, MSPE, residual plots) all evaluate performance over the observed distribution, not over the target population's distribution. If the two populations have divergent covariate profiles, your model validation is measuring the wrong thing.

The Diagnostic Blindspot

Performance metrics like mean squared prediction error are computed over the source study's covariate distribution. When the target population lies in a different region of covariate space, these metrics offer no guarantee of extrapolation validity. A model with excellent in-sample fit can produce severely biased extrapolated predictions.

How MAIC's Entropy Balancing Changes the Game

MAIC operates on a fundamentally different principle: instead of modelling the outcome, it directly solves for weights that enforce covariate balance between the source and target studies. This is an entropy balancing approach — a convex optimization that is more stable and robust to model specification than conventional inverse probability weighting.

How MAIC Achieves Cross-Study Covariate Balance

The key diagnostic advantage of MAIC: balance can be directly verified. After computing weights, you simply check whether weighted covariate moments in Study A match the published moments from Study B. Poor balance is a transparent, quantifiable signal of residual bias — something parametric G-computation cannot offer.

First vs Second Order Moments

A subtle but important point: standard MAIC only balances first-order moments (means). If the outcome model involves squared terms or non-linear interactions — as Vo demonstrated in his simulation — matching on means alone leaves residual confounding. Extending MAIC to balance second-order moments (variances, squared covariates) provides additional protection, at the cost of reduced effective sample size and a greater risk of convergence failure.

Key Insight

For entropy balancing (MAIC), the level of covariate balance achieved is directly observable and verifiable. For outcome modeling (G-computation), the adequacy of the model in non-overlapping regions is fundamentally unverifiable without additional data.

Section 02

Model-Based Extrapolation: Undesirable, But Sometimes Necessary

The criticism of G-computation is well-founded: it extrapolates. But what if extrapolation is the only option? This is not a defence of sloppy modelling — it's an acknowledgement of the structural realities of HTA decision-making.

The Reimbursement Imperative

This is the crux of the issue: HTA bodies do not have the luxury of saying "the populations are too different — we can't compare." Reimbursement decisions must be made. Patients are waiting. Economic models need to be populated.

When covariate distributions between the IPD trial and the aggregate data trial are divergent enough that MAIC cannot find a valid weighting solution (convergence failure), the analyst faces a stark choice: use G-computation with its extrapolation risk, or submit no adjusted estimate at all. The latter is almost always worse.

The Honest Uncertainty Problem

Remiro-Azócar et al. acknowledge Vo's valid point: MAIC makes uncertainty more visible through extreme weights, while G-computation can appear deceptively precise. Interval estimates from G-computation do not automatically reflect extrapolation uncertainty. Analysts and reviewers should treat precision from G-computation with appropriate scepticism when overlap is limited.

The Covariate Selection Dilemma

A further tension: in anchored comparisons, NICE guidance suggests excluding covariates with poor overlap that are not effect modifiers. But identifying effect modifiers in practice is difficult — effect modification is scale-dependent, and for novel therapies, the clinical evidence base on drivers of treatment effect heterogeneity is often thin. Excluding weak effect modifiers to improve overlap can itself induce bias, as Vo's second simulation demonstrated.

There is an unavoidable trade-off:

  • More covariates adjusted: greater plausibility of conditional constancy (transportability), but worse overlap and ESS loss.
  • Fewer covariates adjusted: better overlap and precision, but greater risk of residual confounding if excluded covariates are in fact effect modifiers.
Section 03

The Perils of Data-Adaptive G-Computation

Given that parametric models are fragile, it seems natural to reach for machine learning — let the data decide the functional form. This is the appeal of data-adaptive G-computation: methods like lasso, random forests, or neural networks fitted to the outcome model. But this route has its own hidden traps.

Convergence Rates: Parametric vs Data-Adaptive Estimators

Three Compounding Problems

  • 1

    Slow convergence rates. Data-adaptive estimators converge to the truth, but at a rate slower than the parametric n½ rate. In the small-to-moderate sample sizes typical of HTA submissions, this manifests as larger-than-expected bias and poor coverage of confidence intervals.

  • 2

    Regularisation-induced bias. Methods like lasso regularise heavily to avoid overfitting — this is a feature for prediction, but a bug for causal inference. The lasso systematically down-weights or eliminates variables that are weak effect modifiers but correlated with study assignment, introducing confounding bias.

  • 3

    Non-standard asymptotic distributions. Parametric estimators enjoy the central limit theorem — asymptotic normality allows straightforward bootstrap confidence intervals. Data-adaptive estimators have irregular asymptotic distributions that cannot easily be derived, making uncertainty quantification theoretically unsound with standard bootstrapping.

What About BART?

The authors suggest Bayesian Additive Regression Trees (BART) as a promising alternative. BART is highly flexible, requires minimal tuning, and by sampling directly from the posterior distribution of the treatment effect, sidesteps the bootstrap problem — principled uncertainty quantification is built-in.

BART: Promising but Not a Panacea

BART regularises heavily (like the lasso) to prevent overfitting, which can induce bias in treatment effect estimation. Crucially, regression trees are piecewise step functions: they partition the covariate space into disjoint rectangles and extrapolate beyond the overlap region using a constant value. Under poor overlap — exactly the scenario where G-computation gets called into service — BART's extrapolation can be particularly crude, and empirical evidence from causal inference literature shows poor coverage in these scenarios.

The message is clear: data-adaptive methods do not solve the extrapolation problem. They solve the parametric misspecification problem, but introduce new challenges around convergence, valid inference, and their own form of extrapolation failure.

Section 04

The Promise of Doubly-Robust Adjustment

The most exciting methodological frontier is the doubly-robust (DR) framework. These methods combine a trial assignment model (how patients end up in each study) with an outcome model (what drives the outcome), and critically, only need one of the two to be correctly specified to achieve unbiased estimation.

Doubly-Robust Augmented Weighting: Two Paths to Consistency

The Slow Convergence Dividend

Here is the most technically valuable insight in the paper. In a doubly-robust framework, the product of convergence rates is what matters. If both working models converge at a subparametric rate r, the DR estimator can still achieve the fast n½ rate — provided the product r × r ≥ n½. This means data-adaptive methods are legitimately accommodated within DR frameworks in a way that they are not in singly-robust G-computation.

Why Doubly-Robust + Machine Learning Works Better

The slow convergence of machine learning estimators (a bug in standalone G-computation) becomes tolerable inside a DR framework, because the overall convergence rate is determined by the product of the two sub-model rates. This is the mathematical justification for using Super Learner or similar ensemble methods within augmented weighting estimators for ITCs.

Outstanding Challenges

The authors are careful not to oversell the DR approach. Several challenges remain:

  • The Donsker condition: Asymptotic normality of DR estimators (which enables simple variance estimation) relies on an unverifiable regularity condition from empirical process theory. This condition may fail in high dimensions, restricting the complexity of machine learning methods that can be safely used.
  • Cross-fitting: The Donsker condition can be relaxed using sample-splitting (cross-fitting), but this introduces additional finite-sample variance and must be iterated many times to remove dependence on the random split — computationally intensive when closed-form variance expressions are unavailable.
  • Limited IPD access: Many DR methods require individual patient data on both sides of the comparison. In typical HTA settings, only one trial provides IPD; the other provides aggregate data only. Tractable approximations to the efficient influence function under aggregate data constraints remain an open research question.
Practical Guidance

A Decision Framework for HTA Analysts

Translating the methodological debate into practice, here is a structured decision framework based on the key considerations in the paper:

Method Selection Flowchart for Covariate-Adjusted ITC

Practical Checklist for HTA Submissions

  • 1

    Assess overlap first. Visualise covariate distributions of both studies. Compute standardised mean differences. If major imbalances exist, MAIC's effective sample size will be severely reduced. Document this.

  • 2

    Attempt MAIC as primary analysis. Entropy balancing is more bias-robust. Report balance diagnostics (standardised mean differences before and after weighting). Extreme weights should trigger an explicit discussion of uncertainty.

  • 3

    Use G-computation as a supportive/sensitivity analysis. Fit a parametric outcome model; document model selection explicitly and transparently. Acknowledge that model performance cannot be validated in non-overlapping regions.

  • 4

    If G-computation is the primary analysis (due to MAIC convergence failure), acknowledge the extrapolation explicitly and provide probabilistic sensitivity analyses that propagate model uncertainty into the economic model.

  • 5

    Consider doubly-robust approaches. Where IPD is available for both studies, augmented weighting estimators with Super Learner-based nuisance models represent the current methodological frontier. Engage with statistical experts early.

Synthesis

Conclusions: No Free Lunch, Many Trade-Offs

The methodological debate between MAIC and G-computation is not a debate about which method is correct — it is a debate about which failure mode is more acceptable in which context. Both methods fail when their assumptions break down. The analyst's job is to understand how each method fails and whether that failure mode is tolerable given the decision context.

Future gold standard

"Precision and efficiency under no failures of assumptions should not be the only characteristics determining the choice of a suitable estimator. The level of bias reduction due to covariate adjustment is easier to assess for weighting than for outcome modeling."

Remiro-Azócar, Heath & Baio — Research Synthesis Methods, 2023

What the paper ultimately advocates for is a methodological humility that is sometimes absent from HTA submissions: the recognition that all covariate adjustment methods rest on untestable assumptions, that the appropriateness of those assumptions varies by context, and that transparent, honest uncertainty quantification is as important as point estimate precision in supporting robust health technology decisions.

The development of core patient characteristic sets — standardised lists of prognostic covariates to be collected and reported across therapeutic areas — could materially reduce the problem of unavailable or inconsistently defined influential covariates, which no statistical method can overcome.

The Bottom Line for HTA Practitioners

There is no universally superior method. MAIC's bias transparency makes it the natural starting point. G-computation is a pragmatic tool when MAIC breaks down, but requires honest acknowledgement of extrapolation. Doubly-robust estimators represent the methodological direction of travel. Whichever method you choose: show your diagnostics, document your assumptions, and propagate your uncertainty all the way through to the economic model.
