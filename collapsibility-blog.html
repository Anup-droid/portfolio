Why Collapsibility Is the Hidden Fault Line in Indirect Treatment Comparisons

Non-collapsible effect measures like odds ratios and hazard ratios are standard currency in HTA submissions. But when effect modification enters the picture, marginal and conditional estimates can recommend opposite treatments — with neither being wrong.

HTA
HTA Methods Desk Senior Statistician · Evidence Synthesis
| ~12 min read · June 2025 | Based on Phillippo et al. (2025) RSM

When effect modification is present alongside non-collapsible effect measures (odds ratios, hazard ratios), population-average conditional and marginal estimands can produce conflicting treatment rankings. A single treatment decision based on one estimand will either disadvantage the majority of patients or produce a worse expected event count overall. Multilevel network meta-regression (ML-NMR) is currently the only population-adjustment method capable of producing both estimates in any target population of interest.

Setting the Scene: What is Non-Collapsibility?

Every HTA statistician working with binary or time-to-event outcomes has chosen between odds ratios, hazard ratios, risk ratios, and risk differences. That choice is not merely a matter of convention — it has deep structural consequences for how treatment effects are synthesised, compared, and ultimately acted upon.

An effect measure is non-collapsible when the population-average marginal effect cannot be expressed as a weighted average of the individual- or subgroup-specific conditional effects.[1,2] The logit and probit link functions — used in logistic regression — produce non-collapsible log odds ratios. Similarly, the Cox proportional hazards model produces non-collapsible log hazard ratios. These are the dominant models in clinical trial analysis and therefore in HTA evidence synthesis.

Key Insight

Non-collapsibility is not a model misspecification issue. It is a mathematical consequence of modelling a bounded outcome (probabilities must lie between 0 and 1). Switching to a collapsible measure like a risk difference avoids this, but risks impossible predictions and induces spurious treatment–covariate interactions.

The practical upshot: conditioning on a prognostic covariate changes the treatment effect estimate and its interpretation, even when there is no interaction between treatment and covariate. This has been well-documented in the literature.[3] What is far less discussed — and the core contribution of Phillippo et al. (2025)[4] — is what happens when effect modification is also present.

Two Estimands, Two Decision Questions

Before we can appreciate the problem, we need clarity on what we are actually estimating. The distinction between conditional and marginal estimands is conceptual, not merely statistical.

The Population-Average Conditional Estimand

The population-average conditional treatment effect dab(P) is the average of individual-level treatment effects over the covariate distribution of the population P, taken on the linear predictor scale (e.g., log odds ratio scale):

d_ab(P) = ∫ γ_ab(x) · f⁽ᴾ⁾(x) dx
Equation (4) — Phillippo et al. 2025

This answers the question: "Which treatment has the greatest average effect for individuals in this population?" It depends only on the distribution of effect modifiers, not on baseline risk or prognostic factors.

The Population-Average Marginal Estimand

The population-average marginal treatment effect Δab(P) is obtained by first computing the average event probability on each treatment, then comparing them:

Δ_ab(P) = g(π̄_b(P)) − g(π̄_a(P)) where π̄_k(P) = ∫ g⁻¹(η_k(x)) · f⁽ᴾ⁾(x) dx
Equations (6)–(7) — Phillippo et al. 2025

This answers a fundamentally different question: "Which treatment minimises the marginal average event probability in this population?" Critically, this estimand depends on baseline risk, prognostic factors, and the full covariate distribution — not just the effect modifiers.

Conditional Estimand d_ab(P)

Averages individual treatment effects on the linear predictor scale. Answers: which treatment is best on average for each individual? Stable to changes in baseline risk.

Marginal Estimand Δ_ab(P)

Compares average event probabilities on the probability scale. Answers: which treatment minimises overall event burden? Sensitive to baseline risk and prognostic factor distribution.

"The population-average conditional and marginal effects weigh up effectiveness over the population differently… The population-average marginal effects weigh up the expected number of events overall." — Phillippo et al. (2025), Research Synthesis Methods

The Conflict: When Rankings Diverge

The key result of Phillippo et al. is that, when effect modification is present, these two estimands can produce conflicting treatment rankings. This is not a statistical artefact — it reflects two genuinely different and valid decision questions that happen to disagree.

Consider a worked example with three treatments A, B, C and a single covariate x (uniformly distributed between −1 and 1) that is both prognostic and effect-modifying. With model parameters μ = 0, β₁ = −1, β₂,B = −3, β₂,C = −1, γB = −4, γC = −3, the following results emerge:

Figure 1. Individual-level log odds ratios γAB(x) (teal) and γAC(x) (red) for treatments B and C vs. A, across the covariate distribution. The curves cross at x = −0.5 (gold dot). Dashed horizontals show population-average conditional effects. For x < −0.5 (25% of the population), C is individually superior; for x ≥ −0.5 (75%), B is individually superior — yet the marginal estimand favours C overall. Adapted from Phillippo et al. (2025), Figure 1.

The numerical result is stark: the population-average conditional estimand gives dAB = −4 and dAC = −3, making B the better treatment. The population-average marginal estimand gives ΔAB = −2.36 and ΔAC = −2.49, making C the better treatment.[4]

The Painful Trade-off

Choosing C (based on marginal effects) minimises the total number of events, but 75% of the population would do better on B. Choosing B (based on conditional effects) maximises individual average benefit, but results in more events overall. There is no treatment that simultaneously satisfies both decision criteria.

Why Marginal Rankings Are Unstable

A further complication is that population-average marginal treatment rankings depend not just on effect modifiers, but also on baseline risk and the distribution of purely prognostic factors — neither of which alter individual treatment effects in any way.

Figure 2. Population-average conditional (solid) and marginal (dashed) log odds ratios for treatments B and C vs. A, across a range of baseline risk values. Conditional effects are constant — they depend only on effect modifier distribution. Marginal effects shift with baseline risk and can change ranks (gold dot). Adapted from Phillippo et al. (2025), Figure 2.

This is a striking finding with direct implications for HTA submissions. A marginal treatment effect estimate derived from a trial population — say from MAIC or STC — cannot be transported to a different target population with a different baseline risk, even if all covariate means are identical. The marginal estimate is fundamentally population-specific in a way that the conditional estimate is not.

HTA Implication

MAIC and STC produce marginal estimates that are specific to the aggregate study population. Applying these to a NICE decision target population with different background event rates may give numerically different — and potentially differently-ranked — results. This is not bias in the statistical sense; it is the correct answer to the wrong question.

Survival Outcomes: An Even More Complex Picture

For time-to-event outcomes analysed with proportional hazards models, the issues compound. Hazard ratios are non-collapsible, and the population-average marginal hazard ratio is not only population-specific — it is also time-varying.

Even if individual-level conditional hazard ratios are perfectly constant over time (i.e., proportional hazards holds at the individual level), the marginal hazard ratio will vary over time whenever any covariate — prognostic or effect-modifying — is present in the model. This is a mathematical inevitability, not a modelling failure.

For Survival Analysts

When covariates are present, proportional hazards cannot hold at the marginal level. The common practice of applying a single constant hazard ratio to a baseline survival curve in a Markov model is therefore an approximation that ignores this fundamental property. When effect modification is additionally present, marginal hazard curves from different treatments can cross over time — producing different treatment rankings at different time horizons.

The population-average conditional log hazard ratio remains constant over time, depending only on the distribution of effect-modifying covariates. This makes it a more tractable and transportable quantity for economic modelling, provided the right decision question is being answered.

Comparing the Methods: What Can Each Produce?

Different population-adjustment methods target different estimands and are applicable to different target populations. Understanding these distinctions is essential for selecting the right method for any given HTA problem.

Method Estimand Target Population
Standard NMA / Indirect comparison Marginal Average of included studies
IPD network meta-regression Conditional or Marginal Any
ML-NMR Conditional or Marginal Any
MAIC Marginal Single aggregate study
STC (plug-in means) Neither (biased) Single aggregate study
STC (simulation / G-computation) Marginal Single aggregate study
Network meta-interpolation (NMI) Neither (biased) Any (with bias)

The critical limitation for MAIC, STC, and standard NMA is that they can only produce marginal estimates — and only for a specific study population. This means they cannot provide the conditional estimates needed to diagnose whether a rank conflict exists, nor produce estimates relevant to a different decision target population.

ML-NMR is the only currently available population-adjustment method that can produce both conditional and marginal estimates in any target population of interest.[5] It does this by combining individual-level conditional modelling (producing conditional estimands directly) with marginalisation over the target population covariate distribution (producing marginal estimands).

A Pragmatic Framework for Decision-Making

Phillippo et al. do not argue that one estimand is universally superior. Instead, they offer a pragmatic framework for navigating the conflict when it arises.

1

Pre-specify the target population and estimand

In line with NICE TSD 18 and the NICE HTE Manual, the choice of effect modifiers, target population, and estimand type should be pre-specified with clinical justification — not selected post-hoc to favour a particular outcome.

2

Use ML-NMR to produce both conditional and marginal estimates

ML-NMR is the only method that can produce both in any target population. This is a prerequisite for the diagnostic step that follows.

3

Check for rank conflict between estimands

If rankings agree: no substantial conflict. A single treatment decision is defensible for the whole population. If rankings disagree: the two decision questions are irreconcilable with a single population-level decision.

4

Consider subgroup decisions when conflict is present

Identify the key effect modifier(s) driving the rank reversal. Plot individual treatment effects against each covariate, holding others at population means. Select clinically meaningful cut-points. Verify that subgroup-specific conditional and marginal rankings agree within each subgroup.

5

For cost-effectiveness: integrate net benefit over the population

Markov models and decision trees based on average event probabilities do not account for patient heterogeneity. When effect modification is present, a discrete event simulation that constructs net benefit as a function of individual event probabilities is the theoretically correct approach, even if less commonly implemented.

One important clarification: the choice of a subgroup decision strategy is non-trivial. Subgroup analyses have limited power to detect interactions, carry a risk of spurious findings when many candidates are explored, and reduce precision within subgroups. The authors appropriately note these caveats while still advocating for the diagnostic use of rank conflict as a signal that subgroup decisions may be worth pursuing.

What This Means for NICE Submissions

The practical implications for HTA practitioners submitting to NICE — or advising on such submissions — are considerable.

For sponsors: If your submission includes an indirect comparison or population-adjusted estimate using MAIC or STC, you are limited to marginal estimates in the aggregate study population. This means you cannot diagnose whether a rank conflict exists, and your estimates may not be transportable to the NICE decision population. ML-NMR, where feasible, offers substantially more analytical flexibility.

For assessors: When reviewing population-adjusted submissions, it is worth asking whether the submitted estimates are relevant to the NHS target population — not just the population of the comparator trial. Marginal estimates from MAIC or STC are not interchangeable across populations with different baseline risks, even if covariate means are similar.

For economic modellers: The use of a time-invariant hazard ratio applied to a baseline survival curve is a strong simplifying assumption that may be particularly problematic in the presence of population adjustment. When ML-NMR has been used to estimate individual-level survival curves, these should be used directly in the economic model, rather than being summarised to a single constant hazard ratio.

Bottom Line for HTA

Collapsibility is not a technicality — it is central to whether your treatment effect estimates answer the decision question that a payer or HTA body is actually asking. When effect modification is present (which it frequently is), using the wrong estimand or an estimand from the wrong population can lead to a treatment recommendation that is genuinely suboptimal for patients or for the health system. The field now has the methodological tools, principally ML-NMR, to do better.

Key Terms

Marginal Estimand Comparison of average event probabilities. Answers: which treatment minimises overall events? Population-specific.
